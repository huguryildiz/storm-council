# 04 Contradiction Ledger

## Consensus

The council agrees that pure laissez-faire is too weak for exposed workers, while a blanket tax or broad slowdown is not supported by the retrieved evidence. The strongest path is targeted transition support, tax-neutrality reform, monitoring, and sector-specific safeguards.

## Collective Blind Spot

No lens found evaluated primary evidence that a broad AI automation tax has protected employment in practice. This is recorded as `X-008` and as the run-local claim `C-026`.

## Conflicts

### X-001 - Broad AI tax versus targeted tax reform

- Claims: C-006, C-007, C-008, C-025
- Relationship: tension
- Evidence balance: mixed
- Resolution status: partially_resolved
- Why it matters: The decision turns on whether taxation should slow adoption itself or correct narrower distortions and fund transition support.
- Next question: Can a country define a narrow automation-bias correction without taxing augmenting AI uses?
- Decisive missing evidence: Country-specific tax-base design with incidence analysis and evidence on affected worker outcomes.

### X-002 - High AI exposure versus limited current observed disruption

- Claims: C-003, C-004, C-005, C-013, C-022
- Relationship: scope_difference
- Evidence balance: mixed
- Resolution status: unresolved
- Why it matters: Exposure may justify preparation, but current outcomes do not yet justify an emergency economywide brake.
- Next question: Which occupations show both high AI exposure and measured displacement after adoption?
- Decisive missing evidence: Longitudinal occupation-level adoption, employment, wage, and task-use data through the next adoption wave.

### X-003 - Labor-market adjustment versus distributional harm

- Claims: C-016, C-018, C-020, C-024
- Relationship: tension
- Evidence balance: mixed
- Resolution status: partially_resolved
- Why it matters: Aggregate adaptation can coexist with serious losses for exposed workers and regions.
- Next question: Which workers and places face persistent losses from AI adoption rather than temporary transition costs?
- Decisive missing evidence: Worker-level panel evidence linking AI adoption to earnings, displacement, retraining, and regional outcomes.

### X-004 - Productivity gains versus job protection

- Claims: C-009, C-014, C-024, C-025
- Relationship: tension
- Evidence balance: supports_b
- Resolution status: partially_resolved
- Why it matters: A broad slowdown can protect some jobs while giving up productivity gains and augmenting uses.
- Next question: Which policy package preserves useful AI adoption while compensating displaced workers?
- Decisive missing evidence: Microsimulation comparing a broad automation tax, tax-neutrality reform, wage insurance, and training under plausible AI adoption scenarios.

### X-005 - Administrative feasibility of taxing AI automation

- Claims: C-011, C-012, C-015, C-026
- Relationship: evidence_gap
- Evidence balance: supports_a
- Resolution status: unresolved
- Why it matters: A policy that cannot define the taxable event will be hard to enforce and easy to distort.
- Next question: Has any jurisdiction implemented an evaluated automation-tax base that distinguishes substitution from augmentation?
- Decisive missing evidence: Primary legislation, administrative guidance, and evaluation data for an implemented AI automation tax.

### X-006 - Robot-tax theory versus AI-specific fiscal guidance

- Claims: C-006, C-008, C-023
- Relationship: evidence_conflict
- Evidence balance: mixed
- Resolution status: partially_resolved
- Why it matters: Theory can support temporary robot taxes, while fiscal guidance warns that AI-specific taxes may be poorly targeted.
- Next question: Can the robot-tax model be mapped to generative AI tasks without losing its assumptions?
- Decisive missing evidence: A generative-AI-specific model with task complementarity, fiscal incidence, and adoption response calibrated to current data.

### X-007 - Historical adaptation versus AI novelty

- Claims: C-016, C-018, C-021, C-022
- Relationship: scope_difference
- Evidence balance: mixed
- Resolution status: unresolved
- Why it matters: Historical adaptation lowers panic, but cognitive task exposure may change distribution and timing.
- Next question: Does generative AI create different adjustment lags or worker bargaining effects than earlier automation waves?
- Decisive missing evidence: Comparative evidence on AI adoption, task change, wages, and bargaining power across exposed occupations.

### X-008 - Evidence for an implemented broad AI slowdown

- Claims: C-006, C-026, C-025
- Relationship: evidence_gap
- Evidence balance: supports_a
- Resolution status: unresolved
- Why it matters: A recommendation to tax or slow AI adoption would need evidence that such a policy works and is not too blunt.
- Next question: Where is the evaluated primary evidence for a broad AI automation tax protecting jobs?
- Decisive missing evidence: A government evaluation or credible quasi-experimental study of a broad AI automation tax or slowdown rule.
