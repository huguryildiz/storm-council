# 05 Source-Mapped Synthesis

## Executive Summary

Do not use a blanket tax or broad slowdown on AI-driven automation as the default jobs policy. The retrieved evidence supports a narrower package: remove tax distortions that favor labor-replacing capital, fund transition support and wage insurance, invest in worker mobility and training, monitor exposed occupations, and reserve slowing tools for specific safety, antitrust, procurement, or sectoral cases. Pure laissez-faire is also too weak because industrial-robot evidence and policy reports show distributional harms and transition needs.

## Decision Context

The decision is whether governments should protect jobs by taxing or slowing AI-driven automation, or let labor markets adjust. The answer must preserve productivity gains where AI augments work, while protecting workers and regions exposed to displacement.

## Strongest Evidence-Backed Findings

1. **Automation can harm exposed workers and places.** `C-001`, `C-020` are supported by `S-001` and `S-009`. Academic and historian lenses support this; the practitioner lens asks for AI-specific measurement.
2. **AI exposure is large but not the same as realized job loss.** `C-003`, `C-004`, `C-021`, and `C-022` are supported by `S-003`, `S-004`, `S-005`, and `S-010`. Academic and skeptic lenses support the caveat; the economist lens notes that exposure still justifies preparation.
3. **Broad AI-specific taxes are weakly supported by the retrieved fiscal evidence.** `C-006`, `C-009`, `C-011`, `C-015`, and `C-026` support this finding. Economist, practitioner, and skeptic lenses converge; `C-008` keeps a narrower theoretical robot-tax case alive.
4. **The strongest package is targeted transition support plus tax-neutrality reform.** `C-010`, `C-014`, `C-017`, `C-024`, and `C-025` support this. Historian and practitioner lenses emphasize institutions and delivery.

## Main Disagreements

- `X-001`: Robot-tax theory and automation-tax reform conflict with IMF guidance against special AI taxes. Resolution leans toward narrow tax-neutrality reform rather than a blanket slowdown.
- `X-002`: High exposure estimates conflict with current limited observed disruption. This remains unresolved because adoption lags can hide medium-term risk.
- `X-005` and `X-008`: Administrative feasibility and evaluated broad-tax evidence remain open gaps.

- `C-006` (0.86, economist, supported): The IMF fiscal-policy note recommends avoiding special AI taxes as a broad way to reduce automation speed.
- `C-014` (0.86, practitioner, supported): The IMF fiscal guidance emphasizes unemployment insurance, wage insurance, labor-market programs, education, and training as transition tools.

- `C-024` (0.84, skeptic, supported): A policy of letting labor markets adjust without intervention is weakly supported because several sources identify distributional harms and transition needs.
- `C-002` (0.82, academic, supported): The task-based framework separates labor displacement from new task creation when judging automation.
- `C-020` (0.82, historian, supported): Laissez-faire adjustment is weak for exposed workers and places when automation shocks reduce local wages or employment.
- `C-025` (0.82, skeptic, supported): A defensible package combines tax-neutrality reform, transition support, and sector-specific safeguards rather than a blanket automation tax.
- `C-004` (0.80, academic, supported): The ILO analysis finds that generative AI has a larger potential for task augmentation than for full occupation automation in many jobs.
- `C-010` (0.80, economist, supported): Transition support funded through broader fiscal tools is more aligned with the IMF guidance than a blunt adoption brake.
- `C-016` (0.80, historian, supported): The MIT Work of the Future report says that 63 percent of US employment in 2018 was in occupational titles not active in 1940.
- `C-007` (0.78, economist, supported): Acemoglu, Manera, and Restrepo argue that US tax rules favor capital over labor and can encourage excess automation in their model.
- `C-017` (0.78, historian, supported): The MIT report argues that labor-market outcomes depend on institutions, education, standards, and public policy choices.
- `C-005` (0.77, academic, supported): The OECD reports limited early evidence that AI has reduced aggregate labor demand.
- `C-011` (0.76, practitioner, supported): Defining taxable AI-driven automation is administratively hard because AI exposure varies by task, occupation, and use case.
- `C-023` (0.76, skeptic, supported): Robot-tax models are not direct implementation blueprints for generative AI policy.
- `C-012` (0.75, practitioner, supported): If government slows adoption, the policy needs measurement rules that distinguish displacement risk from augmentation and productivity uses.
- `C-009` (0.74, economist, supported): A broad slowdown policy risks taxing augmenting AI uses along with replacing uses.

## Evidence Gaps

- No evaluated primary source was retrieved showing that a broad AI automation tax has protected employment in practice (`C-026`, `X-008`).
- No country-specific tax-base design in this run separated labor substitution from augmentation with enough precision to support immediate legislation (`X-005`).
- No worker-level panel evidence in this run establishes medium-term AI displacement by occupation and region (`X-002`, `X-003`).

## Decision Options and Trade-Offs

- **A. Blanket AI automation tax or slowdown - weak.** It is administratively hard and risks taxing augmenting uses (`C-006`, `C-009`, `C-011`, `X-005`).
- **B. Pure labor-market adjustment - weak.** It under-protects exposed workers and places (`C-001`, `C-020`, `C-024`).
- **C. Targeted transition compact - strongest retrieved support.** Pair tax-neutrality reform, worker support, monitoring, and sectoral safeguards (`C-010`, `C-014`, `C-025`).
- **D. Narrow temporary tax in a defined sector - conditional.** Consider only with a measurable displacement base, incidence analysis, and sunset review (`C-008`, `C-023`, `X-001`).

## Recommended Next Actions

1. Audit current tax rules for capital-labor neutrality before proposing an AI-specific tax (`C-007`, `C-010`, `X-001`).
2. Fund unemployment insurance, wage insurance pilots, labor-market programs, and training before slowing useful adoption (`C-014`, `C-024`).
3. Build a monitoring panel for AI adoption, task change, wages, displacement, and regions (`C-003`, `C-013`, `C-022`, `X-002`).
4. Require any proposed automation tax to define the taxable event, affected workers, incidence, exemptions, and sunset test (`C-011`, `C-015`, `C-026`, `X-005`).
5. Use sector-specific slowing only where safety, market power, public procurement, or local displacement evidence justifies it (`C-025`, `X-008`).

## Hidden Connection

The same evidence that weakens a blanket AI tax also weakens laissez-faire. AI effects are heterogeneous: some tasks are augmented, some workers face displacement risk, and some impacts are delayed. That pushes policy toward targeted insurance and tax-neutrality reform rather than either a universal brake or passive adjustment.

## Frontier Question

Can governments build a worker-level AI impact monitoring system fast enough to trigger targeted support before exposed workers experience persistent wage or employment losses?
