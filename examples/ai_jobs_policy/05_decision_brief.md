# 05 Decision Brief

## Bottom Line

Do not adopt a blanket tax or broad slowdown on AI-driven automation as the default jobs policy. Do not rely on laissez-faire adjustment either. The best-supported path is a targeted transition compact: tax-neutrality reform, worker income support, training and mobility programs, live monitoring of exposed occupations, and sector-specific safeguards where evidence justifies them.

## Findings That Matter

- Automation can reduce employment and wages for exposed workers and places (`C-001`, `C-020`).
- AI exposure is large, but exposure is not a realized job-loss forecast (`C-003`, `C-004`, `C-021`).
- IMF fiscal guidance weighs against special AI taxes as a broad brake (`C-006`).
- Robot-tax theory keeps a narrower, temporary tax option on the table, but only under scoped assumptions (`C-008`, `C-023`).
- Current retrieval found no evaluated primary evidence for a broad AI automation tax protecting employment in practice (`C-026`).

## Recommendation

Choose Option C: targeted transition compact. Its evidence strength is moderate-to-strong because it is supported across IMF fiscal guidance, robot and automation evidence, historical institutional evidence, and administrative feasibility concerns (`C-010`, `C-014`, `C-017`, `C-024`, `C-025`).

## What Would Change This

- Primary evaluation evidence that a broad AI automation tax protected workers with acceptable productivity and compliance costs.
- Worker-level evidence that AI adoption is producing fast persistent displacement that transition supports cannot address.
- A workable legal definition of AI-driven labor substitution that avoids taxing augmentation.

## Live Disagreements

- `X-001`: Whether robot-tax theory justifies a tax instrument or only tax-neutrality reform.
- `X-002`: Whether current limited disruption is reassuring or just a lagging indicator.
- `X-005`: Whether a taxable AI automation base can be administered.
- `X-008`: Whether evaluated broad-tax evidence exists outside this run.

## Caveat

This is a source-mapped decision brief, not legal, fiscal, or labor-market advice. It should support, not replace, domain experts and accountable public decision-making.
