# 02 Perspective Scan: Five Research Lenses

The run uses Council Mode because the decision is policy-relevant, contested, and recommendation-seeking. The lenses below are research charters, not independent persona answers.

## Practitioner

**Role and focus.** Tests whether a tax or slowdown can be defined, measured, enforced, and updated without blocking useful AI. Privileges implementation detail, compliance costs, and agency capacity.

**Priority questions.**
- What is the taxable event?
- How are augmentation and substitution separated?
- What exemptions or safe harbors are needed?
- Can worker support be delivered faster than a new tax regime?

**Expected evidence types.**
- administrative guidance
- policy evaluations
- institutional reports
- implementation case studies

**Likely blind spot.** May underweight long-run social harm if the immediate mechanism is hard to administer.

**Conflicts with other lenses.** economist on model-based tax design, skeptic on whether weak implementation evidence blocks action.

**Unique contribution.** A policy can fail because the tax base is vague even when the economic concern is real.

**Escalation triggers.**
- no evaluated AI tax exists
- policy depends on measuring task substitution

## Academic

**Role and focus.** Separates exposure, displacement, complementarity, and task creation. Privileges peer-reviewed estimates and careful scope preservation.

**Priority questions.**
- What does robot evidence actually establish?
- What does AI exposure measure?
- Which claims are forecasts rather than facts?
- How should task creation enter the answer?

**Expected evidence types.**
- peer-reviewed labor economics
- task-based models
- systematic reviews
- institutional exposure reports

**Likely blind spot.** Can underweight urgent administrative and political constraints.

**Conflicts with other lenses.** practitioner on implementation sufficiency, historian on analogy strength.

**Unique contribution.** Exposure metrics are useful for preparation but weak evidence for an immediate broad slowdown.

**Escalation triggers.**
- exposure is treated as realized job loss
- robot evidence is mapped directly to generative AI

## Skeptic

**Role and focus.** Looks for overclaiming, missing counterevidence, vague value judgments, and whether a source entails the exact policy recommendation. Privileges negative evidence and external validity.

**Priority questions.**
- What did this run fail to retrieve?
- Are model results being sold as implementation evidence?
- Does laissez-faire hide value judgments?
- Does a tax proposal have a real tax base?

**Expected evidence types.**
- null-result logs
- scope audits
- policy evaluations
- contradictory evidence

**Likely blind spot.** May discount useful directional theory when direct evaluation is unavailable.

**Conflicts with other lenses.** economist on robot-tax model use, historian on analogies.

**Unique contribution.** The absence of evaluated broad AI-tax evidence is itself a decision-relevant finding.

**Escalation triggers.**
- recommendation rests on abstract-only evidence
- policy evidence is missing

## Economist

**Role and focus.** Evaluates incentives, incidence, productivity, tax neutrality, and redistribution. Privileges models, fiscal-policy guidance, and opportunity cost.

**Priority questions.**
- Does the tax correct a distortion or suppress productivity?
- Who bears the incidence?
- What alternative fiscal tools exist?
- How large is the transition externality?

**Expected evidence types.**
- tax-incidence models
- IMF/OECD fiscal guidance
- labor-market estimates
- welfare models

**Likely blind spot.** May focus on efficiency metrics and understate institutional trust or job-quality concerns.

**Conflicts with other lenses.** historian on social legitimacy, practitioner on feasible tax bases.

**Unique contribution.** The strongest tax case is narrow tax-neutrality reform and transition funding, not a blanket AI brake.

**Escalation triggers.**
- broad tax is proposed without incidence analysis
- transition support is unfunded

## Historian

**Role and focus.** Compares AI to earlier automation waves while checking the limits of analogy. Privileges long-run adjustment, institutional complements, and place-based harms.

**Priority questions.**
- Which past transitions created new work?
- Where did adjustment fail?
- What institutions changed outcomes?
- What makes generative AI different?

**Expected evidence types.**
- historical labor studies
- institutional reports
- systematic reviews
- regional adjustment evidence

**Likely blind spot.** May assume new technology will resemble earlier waves too closely.

**Conflicts with other lenses.** academic on novelty and measurement, skeptic on analogy limits.

**Unique contribution.** Neither panic nor laissez-faire follows from history; institutions determine whether adaptation is tolerable.

**Escalation triggers.**
- aggregate adaptation is used to dismiss harmed workers
- AI novelty is used to dismiss past evidence
