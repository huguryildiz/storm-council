# 04 Council Deliberation

Council Mode was bounded to two rounds. Each lens made one structured move per round on the highest-impact conflicts.

- **R1 / academic / qualification on `C-003`**: Exposure estimates should trigger preparation, not be converted into a job-loss forecast. Refs: C-003, C-021.

- **R1 / economist / qualification on `C-008`**: Robot-tax theory supports a temporary model result, but not a blanket AI tax without incidence and tax-base design. Refs: C-008, C-023.

- **R1 / practitioner / challenge on `C-011`**: A broad automation tax lacks an administrable boundary unless legislation can separate substitution from augmentation. Refs: C-011, X-005.

- **R1 / historian / qualification on `C-016`**: History supports adaptation, but exposed workers need institutions and not just time. Refs: C-016, C-020.

- **R1 / skeptic / request_for_evidence on `C-026`**: Before slowing AI broadly, show evaluated primary evidence that the instrument protected jobs in practice. Refs: C-026, X-008.

- **R2 / academic / qualification on `C-013`**: Near-term aggregate tracking does not remove medium-term occupational risk. Refs: C-013, C-022.

- **R2 / economist / support on `C-010`**: A package of transition support and tax-neutrality reform preserves useful adoption while funding adjustment. Refs: C-010, C-025.

- **R2 / practitioner / support on `C-014`**: Worker-facing tools are more operationally legible than taxing a vague technology category. Refs: C-014, C-015.

- **R2 / historian / support on `C-019`**: Institutions, not blanket slowing, are the recurring mechanism for making technology transitions tolerable. Refs: C-019, C-017.

- **R2 / skeptic / reframing on `C-025`**: The decision is not tax versus market adjustment; it is blanket brake versus targeted public risk management. Refs: C-025, X-001.
