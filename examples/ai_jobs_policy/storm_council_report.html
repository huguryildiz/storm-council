01Bottom line

Do not use a blanket AI automation tax or broad slowdown as the default jobs policy, and do not rely on laissez-faire adjustment. Use a targeted transition compact: tax-neutrality reform, worker support, monitoring, and sector-specific safeguards where evidence warrants them.

02The question and the council

Decision Question

Should governments tax or slow AI-driven automation to protect jobs, or let labor markets adjust?

Acting Audience

A national economic-policy team deciding whether to pursue a broad AI automation tax or slowdown rule, a laissez-faire adjustment posture, or a targeted transition package.

Why It Matters

The policy has a two-sided risk. Moving too slowly could expose workers and regions to avoidable displacement, wage pressure, and bargaining-power loss. Moving too bluntly could tax productivity-improving or worker-augmenting AI uses and reduce the fiscal base needed to fund transition support.

Scope

  • Government policy toward AI-driven automation that affects labor demand, wages, job quality, and worker transition.
  • Broad taxes or slowdown rules, narrower tax-neutrality reforms, worker-support packages, and sector-specific safeguards.
  • Evidence from industrial robots, generative AI exposure, fiscal-policy analysis, historical labor adjustment, and current labor-market tracking.

Out of Scope

  • Detailed design of one country's tax code.
  • Safety regulation for frontier AI models except where adoption speed affects labor policy.
  • Firm-level deployment advice.
  • A legal opinion on whether a specific automation tax is permissible.

Time Horizon and Risk Tolerance

The brief is aimed at a 3 to 10 year policy horizon. The risk tolerance is asymmetric: avoid irreversible worker harm and avoid suppressing useful productivity gains without evidence that a broad slowdown works.

Stakeholders

Workers in exposed occupations, unemployed and displaced workers, employers adopting AI, firms building AI systems, taxpayers, fiscal authorities, education and training providers, unions, consumers, and regions with concentrated exposure.

What Would Change the Recommendation

  • Direct evaluation evidence that a broad AI automation tax protected jobs without large productivity or compliance costs.
  • Strong worker-level evidence that AI adoption is producing persistent displacement faster than transition policy can respond.
  • Evidence that current tax rules materially subsidize labor-replacing AI relative to labor or worker-augmenting investment.
  • Evidence that transition support and tax-neutrality reforms fail while a slowdown succeeds.

Known Uncertainties

  • AI exposure estimates are not realized job-loss estimates.
  • Industrial robot evidence is informative but not identical to generative AI.
  • Fiscal incidence of an AI automation tax depends on tax-base design and market structure.
  • Current aggregate indicators may lag occupational and regional harms.

Acceptance Criteria

  • Every factual claim ties to a stable source ID and, where possible, an evidence locator.
  • Major contradictions are logged explicitly rather than averaged away.
  • Recommendations distinguish fact, inference, forecast, and value judgment.
  • High-stakes policy caveats state that the brief supports, not replaces, expert and accountable human decision-making.
