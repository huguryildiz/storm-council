**Topic:** Should reinforcement learning be used for university course timetabling?  
**Audience:** University CIO and registrar (technical decision-makers)  
**Time horizon:** 12-24 months · **Risk tolerance:** low · **Output:** decision brief  
**Orchestration mode:** council

## Decision question
Whether and when reinforcement learning should complement (not replace) CP-SAT/MIP for university course timetabling.

## Why this decision matters
Timetabling errors cascade into room clashes, instructor conflicts, and student appeals. Choosing the wrong solver paradigm risks either missing feasible, auditable schedules or over-investing in ML infrastructure that does not beat established constraint solvers.

## Scope
- University course (not exam) timetabling
- CP-SAT / MIP exact solvers and metaheuristics as the incumbent baseline
- Reinforcement learning as a potential complement

## Exclusions
- Exam timetabling and invigilation rostering
- Commercial vendor selection / procurement
- Student preference elicitation methods

## Key assumptions
- Hard constraints (no clashes, room capacity) must be guaranteed, not approximated.
- Schedules must be explainable enough to handle appeals.
- ML engineering capacity is scarce and must be justified against alternatives.

## Stakeholders
- Registrar / scheduling office
- Students and instructors
- IT / ML engineering
- University leadership (budget owner)

## What would change the decision
- A peer-reviewed head-to-head showing RL beating CP-SAT/MIP on realistic instances.
- A validated disruption simulator enabling reliable RL rescheduling.
- Evidence that rescheduling labor costs dominate total scheduling cost.

## Known uncertainties
- Whether RL generalizes across heterogeneous instance sizes.
- True total cost of ownership of an RL deployment.
- How much soft-objective adaptivity institutions actually need.
